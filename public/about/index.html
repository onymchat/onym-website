Foundation

Privacy is a right,
not a feature.

Onym builds messaging that requires no trust -- not in us, not in any company, not in any server.

Why we exist

Every messenger today asks you to trust their server. Signal trusts AWS. Telegram trusts its own data centers. Apple trusts its own data centers. WhatsApp trusts Meta's. The encryption between users is end-to-end -- but the plumbing always runs on hardware someone else owns and someone else can be compelled to change.

"Trust" sounds harmless until you read the fine print: when governments, courts, lawyers, or new owners come knocking, the messenger's job is to comply. Even when they can't read your messages, they can almost always reveal who you talk to, when, and how often. That's the social graph -- and that's what gets people targeted, profiled, or arrested.

The only way to be sure no one can read your conversations or reconstruct your relationships is to remove the server entirely, and to make the only piece of shared state public, auditable, and beyond any one party's control.

How Onym is built

Identity on device. Your keys, contacts, and message history are generated and stored on your phone. Twelve BIP-39 words restore the lot on another device -- and they leave only when you type them. No account creation, no phone number, no email.

Group state on a public smart contract. The rules of every group -- who can admit, who can remove, who can speak -- live on Stellar via Soroban. Anyone can verify them; no one can override them. The contract is the only thing all members share, and it is public by design.

Relays as dumb pipes. Encrypted message blobs travel over Nostr relays. Run your own. Run someone else's. Switch any time. The transport layer is replaceable, and nothing on it is in the clear.

Open from top to bottom

Every layer is MIT-licensed and on GitHub: onym-ios, onym-android, onym-contracts, the onym-relayer, and the SDKs.

The cryptographic primitives are not novel. BIP-39 (mnemonics), secp256k1 with Schnorr (Nostr identity), Ed25519 (chain anchors), X25519 (sealed inboxes), BLS12-381 (group signatures), PLONK (membership proofs), Poseidon (SNARK-friendly hashes) -- every one studied, deployed, and attacked by cryptographers for decades. We don't roll our own crypto.

If you'd like to read the source, audit the math, or build reproducible binaries, you can. That is the deal.
